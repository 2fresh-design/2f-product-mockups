03  HOW WE'RE WORKING

Building in public.
Evidence, not intuition.

  • 8 INTERVIEWS In-depth interviews with designers, hiring managers, and senior leads. ~60–90 minutes. Transcribed, coded, synthesized across.
  • 12 HYPOTHESES Kill-or-go framework. Some falling, some confirming, others under pressure. None definitively killed yet.

QUOTE 1/6#workaround

"I dropped the job ad and my CV into GPT and chatted with the bot. It told me what I'd forgotten."

Senior designer

The candidate used AI as a career coach for himself — replacing the mentor, colleague, or friends he'd usually turn to. Interview prep is shifting from people to the model.

QUOTE 2/6#standard

"Companies only have two extreme modes — either desperately begging, or passively posting a role and waiting to see who shows up."

Senior designer + former hiring lead

Either frantic candidate hunting, or passive waiting for a pile of CVs. No one has a structured process in between.

QUOTE 3/6#pain

"120 CVs in two weeks. Maybe 15 of them relevant. HR could have filtered this — but didn't."

Hiring lead, small corporate design team

HR as an admin pass-through, not a partner. The lead screens alone, between meetings, before 9 a.m.

QUOTE 4/6#standard

"She laid out the digits from my birth date in a grid and started reading off my traits."

Junior researcher, career switcher after 12 years in IT

A numerology grid as an evaluation tool in a job interview. When standards are missing, people reach for anything.

QUOTE 5/6#validation

"The case study tells you what maturity the company has, or how it approaches design."

Mid-level designer, hiring panel participant

Candidates judge the company by the quality of the brief they're given. When the brief is shallow, good designers apply elsewhere.

QUOTE 6/6#validation

"I don't read CVs. I scan for 30 minutes a day. Even imperfect AI would do better."

Lead of a large corporate design team

A hiring manager with dozens of hires under his belt says it explicitly: pre-vetting has value. Even imperfect.

04  OTHER THINGS WE LEARNED

Things we
didn't expect.

Domain bias
Banks want a banker-designer.
What gets evaluated is the previous client's industry, not the design skill itself. Banking, telco, e-commerce — everyone wants someone from their own world. Design competence steps aside. A risk for a product built on a portable framework.
Root cause in the JD
Bad JD = bad hiring.
A generic job ad pulls in the wrong candidates from the start. When the root cause sits in the brief, interviews, tasks, and assessments only treat the symptoms — they can't save a bad start.

And more — switchers as an invisible segment, case studies as a proxy for the company, AI as pain not just opportunity. There's more than fits here.
